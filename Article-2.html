What Goes In Must Come Out:

Trail Waste Ethics

Nature has no bins—what you carry in,

you must carry out.

Leave the Trail Better Than You Found It

When we venture into the wild, nature offers us peace, clarity, and beauty. But with that gift comes responsibility. Whether you’re hiking, camping, or just enjoying a forest trail, everything you bring with you has an impact—even what you leave behind. This is where trail waste ethics come in: a conscious commitment to leaving no trace, especially when it comes to human and material waste.

What Is Trail Waste?

Trail waste includes anything left behind on a hike—this can be obvious like plastic wrappers and bottles, or less visible but equally harmful things like micro-trash, food scraps, and human waste. Each piece left behind affects wildlife, disrupts ecosystems, and degrades the trail experience for others.

Why It Matters?

In natural environments, waste doesn’t just disappear. Many trails don’t have trash bins or maintenance crews. Even “biodegradable” items like orange peels or tissues can take months or years to decompose and attract wildlife, changing their behavior or making them ill. Human waste, if not handled properly, pollutes water sources, spreads disease, and alters soil chemistry. Leaving waste behind disrespects the very environment we seek to enjoy.

Dealing with Human Waste

When nature calls, here’s how to answer responsibly:

  • Use designated toilet facilities whenever possible.
  • In remote areas, bury your waste in a "cathole": 6-8 inches deep and at least 200 feet away from water sources, trails, or campsites.
  • Pack out used toilet paper and hygiene products in sealed, odor-proof bags.
  • On popular or sensitive trails, use a portable toilet bag ("wag bag") and pack it out. It's required in some areas.

What About Food Waste?

Many hikers assume that food waste like fruit peels or crumbs “isn’t trash.” But in nature, it is.

  • Don’t feed wildlife—intentionally or accidentally.
  • Pack out all food scraps, even biodegradable ones.
  • Use a trash bag or old snack pouch to store waste until you reach proper disposal.

EcoTrails Tip: Carry a Waste Kit

Create your own leave-no-trace kit:

  • Ziplock or dry bag for trash
  • Trowel or small spade for catholes
  • Biodegradable soap & hand sanitizer
  • TP in a waterproof pouch
  • Extra bag for hygiene waste

Make it lightweight and always bring it along.

The Golden Rule: Pack It In, Pack It Out

This principle is the foundation of trail waste ethics. If you brought it, you carry it back—no exceptions. This includes food wrappers, broken gear, wipes, tissues, water bottles, and more. "The trail doesn’t come with a cleanup crew. That’s your job."

Learning trail waste ethics is one step; living them is another. Model good habits. Pick up stray trash. Talk to your hiking group about it. If we all do our part, trails stay wild, clean, and welcoming.
